Origins

Built as a symbol of Japan's postwar rebirth, Tokyo Tower was inspired by the Eiffel Tower — but painted orange and white to meet aviation safety rules. At completion it was the tallest structure in all of Japan.

Completed in 1958, Tokyo Tower stands in the Minato district as one of the most recognisable landmarks in Asia. It houses two observation decks — the Main Deck at 150 metres and the Top Deck at 250 metres — both offering sweeping views across Tokyo and, on clear days, all the way to Mount Fuji.

At its base sits FootTown, a four-storey complex packed with museums, cafés, and shops. Though Tokyo Skytree surpassed it in height in 2012, Tokyo Tower remains the city's most beloved structure — a warm orange glow that has watched over the skyline for over sixty years.

The tower changes colour with the seasons — glowing diamond white in winter and landmark orange the rest of the year. On special occasions it dresses in custom lighting to mark everything from national holidays to charity campaigns. For many Tokyo residents, spotting the tower through a gap in the buildings on their daily commute is a quiet, reassuring reminder of home.
